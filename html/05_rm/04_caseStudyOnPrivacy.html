Case study on privacy

Ricardo works for the records department of his local government as a computer records clerk, where he has access to files of property tax records. For a scientific study, a researcher, Beth, has been granted access to the numerical portion “but not the corresponding names” of some records.

Beth finds some information that she would like to use, but she needs the names and addresses corresponding with certain properties. Beth asks Ricardo to retrieve these names and addresses, so she can contact these people for more information and for permission to do further study.

If Ricardo is not responsible for determining allowable access, should he release the names and addresses?

Ricardo must not release authorization without explicit authorization of the government, to be compliant with privacy policies and laws regarding data privacy and protection, such as the General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) in Europe, the Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act (HIPAA) in the United States for health information, and other similar regulations globally. These laws protect individuals' data privacy and restrict how their personal information can be used and shared.

Suppose Ricardo were responsible for determining allowable access to the files. What ethical issues would be involved in his deciding whether to grant access to Beth?

The ethical considerations include privacy, consent, impact on trust, agency, etc. This is because data is not simply a number on a dataset, but they are individuals’s data that can impact their lives and well-being, and individuals must decide if they want to grant access to third-party individuals for research purposes.

Should Beth be allowed to contact the individuals involved? That is, should the Records department release individuals' names to a researcher? What are the ethical issues for the Records department to consider?

The main ethical concern is the privacy and confidentiality of those who own those records. Releasing their personal information, such as names and contact information, without authorization can violate their privacy rights. Other concerns include consent, transparency, and accountability.

Suppose Beth contacts the individuals to ask their permission, and one-third of them respond giving permission, one-third respond denying permission, and one-third do not respond. Beth claims that at least one-half of the individuals are needed to make a valid study. What options are available to Beth?

Beth might want to consider some of these options:

  1. Try to contact again users who didn’t respond. This way, she can complete the number of individuals needed for her research.
  2. Revisit her study approach. She could redesign her method so fewer individuals are needed.
  3. Look for more participants.
  4. Look for additional data sources.
