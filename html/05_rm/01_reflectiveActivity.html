Reflective activity - Ethics in Computing

I want to start this reflection by mentioning a very inspirational quote.

“Describing computing as a profession is thus linked to the expectation that computing professionals will pay attention to ethics.” (Stahl et al., 2016).

I am currently a software engineering manager working at a technology startup. One of my duties is fraud prevention. In my current role, I act as both, a decision-maker and an individual contributor.

I believe that ethics can be taught, and engineering managers must ensure their teams work with the highest standards of ethics. Additionally, must ensure that every team member understands why ethics are crucial and that it is the responsibility of every team member to promote them.

As Marebene & Hans (2023) mention in their paper, computing professionals, such as software engineers, must act with caution because they have the power to make decisions that can have a huge impact on the well-being of others. This statement is detailed and expanded by Stahl et al. (2016), who mention several ethical issues involving technology, such as privacy of users, autonomy, agency, trust, and credibility. However, Stahl et al. (2016) also mentioned they found during their research that privacy is the most dominant ethical issue among all the papers and articles they reviewed, where privacy was present in 45% of them.

Focusing on privacy and software engineering, Marebene et al. (2023) describe why privacy is crucial when discussing ethical issues. Software is everywhere in the industry, regardless of the nature of businesses, and many organizations use software to make decisions. Moreover, many individuals use software to process information and sensitive data, such as medical records, personal data, photographs, and identity information. It can be dangerous if data of this type is in the wrong hands.

We must comply with the Software Engineering code of ethics as software engineers. All points in the code of ethics are important, and all of them reflect a common sense of working ethically: working for the best interest of the public, working with integrity, promoting ethical approaches when developing software, and continuing to learn about ethics within the computing domain (Gotterbarn et al., 1997). However, in the real world, bad actors always do not care about people and will use their power for their own good.

A great example of this is the use of dark UX patterns to develop products. I talked about this in the Collaborative learning discussion from Unit One (Castro, 2023). While software engineers must comply with the code of ethics and act in their clients' or employers' best interest (Gotterbarn et al., 1997), sometimes clients do not work ethically and request engineering teams work that is against the code of ethics.

Considering the statement above, although everyone should act ethically regardless of their position, professional ethics should always come from leadership positions, and those individuals holding these positions should ensure that their teams work ethically. Can you imagine a client requesting to build software that will harm their clients' privacy? Leadership positions must deny these requests and create politics against them.

Moreover, bad actors can be everywhere, including engineering teams.

In my experience, trusting your team is a powerful tool for building loyalty and trust. However, you cannot know if somebody will stick to the code of ethics. To cover this, it is a best practice to have layers of permissions and log all access to sensitive data.

In my current role, I am responsible for preventing fraud in many ranges, mainly identity theft and account takeover. On a daily basis, we see bad actors everywhere trying to commit fraud: people who have access to sensitive identity information or even financial information, like identity cards (election cards, driving licenses, or passports) or financial statements with historical records of credits. This data type is very sensitive, and in most cases, it comes from companies or organizations with poor security policies, a lack of ethical sense among their team members, and a lack of ethical formation within their leadership teams.

In conclusion, ethics is crucial in the computing profession. As I quoted in the first paragraph of this document, to be considered professionals, we must pay attention to ethics (Stahl et al., 2016). That quote is a powerful message that should be captured in every company’s mission and vision.
