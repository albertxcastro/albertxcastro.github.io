Understanding Artificial Intelligence

What is Artificial Intelligence?

In their book, Artificial Intelligence: A modern approach, Russell, S. & Norvig, P. (2013) try to define what Artificial Intelligence is, using two different dimensions. These two dimensions measure human performance and rationality. Human performance involves thought processes and reasoning: AI is a machine's ability to perform tasks with human intelligence at a certain level. On the other hand, rationality is defined as a machine's ability to act correctly given the inputs from its environment.

To measure a machine's ability to think humanly, Alan Turing proposed the famous Turing Test in 1950. in this test, a human would interrogate a participant with written questions. A machine passes this test if it can convince the interrogator that it is a person and not a machine.

Alternatively, machines' ability to reason relies on several fields, two of the most important philosophy and mathematics. Philosophy poses the questions and sets formal rules to reach valid conclusions. Mathematics sets the limits and decides what can be computable. Moreover, the reasoning connects with the action of the act, which introduces a new concept called "the agent".

An agent is something that can act. Therefore, reasoning agents can act the right way; they can adapt to changes in their environment, manipulate it and make decisions with the available information. In a word, agents are autonomous.

AI then can be defined as the branch of computer science that studies how computers can think, learn and act autonomously, automating tasks that imitate human intelligence.
